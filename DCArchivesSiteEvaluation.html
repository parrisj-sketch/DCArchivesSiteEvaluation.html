DC Archives Site Evaluation

Description of the organization and the information system

The Office of Public Records is a division of the Office of the Secretary. From the Office of the Secretary “The Office of Public Records schedules, collects, stores and manages records of the District government through the services of the District of Columbia Archives, Records Center and the Library of Government Information. These repositories hold a wide array of documents that include administrative, architectural, engineering, fiscal, genealogical, historical and legal records. The office also surveys, appraises and schedules records; accessions records into the Archives, Records Center and Library of Information; services records and research requests from agencies of the government and the public; refiles and relocates records in the repositories; and disposes records that have met the terms and conditions of the schedules.”

Accessing the information in the massive database is fairly simle. The search is mainly a keyword search with the option of the advanced Boolean type search. Along with the keywords pulling relevant searches based on the title, the system they use scans the digitized item and identifies the keyword there as well.

Evaluation of the Existing System

Overall, the system functions as intended with very few hiccups for anyone conducting research. The keyword search and Boolean search work as intended and any issues that arise from conducting a search can be fixed by refining the query with the advanced search tool. As stated previously, the only major setback is the design of the site. It deviates a bit from the a majority of the many dc.gov sites, but it still is bound by the lack of innovation in the user interface of those sites. Based on the usability heuristics of user interface design, the platform falls short in two key ways; error prevention and aesthetic and minimalist design.

Error Prevention

The site is cluttered with lots of information available to the user for them to be able to navigate the massive database. An issue with this is the amount of user knowledge required to fix a mistake made when conducting a search. For people that are inexperienced with using a keyword or Boolean search, it is easy to reach a dead end if a mistake is made on the user end. if the user makes a mistake, there is nothing there to show them how to refine their search to find an answer that is useful to their research.

Aesthetic and Minimalist Design

As stated before, the websites problem with its design is in part, due to the constraints of it being part of the DC Government network of sites. One suggestion I would offer to set itself apart from the monotony that are the dc.gov sites is to present the information on this site like a museum. Pages opening with photos of the many historical figures whose names and likeness are stored in the archive. A future project for the archive would be to orgsnize all the available digitl information into smaller, more digestible collections that allow more casual researchers to use the site. The only drawback to this is finding the balance between an enjoyable user experience, and a site that functions best for historians and researchers

Alternative Solutions

When designing government websites, it is important to have a through line when it comes to aesthetics. The main issue so far as was reviewed previously is that the family of DC government websites seem to be outdated in terms of their user interface design. When looking to the future it is important to consider the heuristic design principles specifically the ones mentioned above to best design A site that people would want to use and feel comfortable using. The only thing you have to worry about is the fine line you walk when creating a repository for historical information. It is important to walk the line between aesthetics and entertainment and being able to properly assess and display historical information in a way that does not distort its importance

Conclusion

The next step would involve changing the layout of all the DC government sites to create some continuity in their design. According to their remote user interface guide and the online style guide, the standard design for the dc government has been used since 2014. The site works well for communicating the necessary information for the constituents of the DC government, but id does not adhere well to principles of design that make websites both informational and easy to use. Updating the user interface guide to fit these design principles will make the experience of using these sites more enjoyable for all who need to navigate them.
